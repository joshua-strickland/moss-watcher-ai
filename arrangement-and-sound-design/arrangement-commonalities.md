# Melodic House Structural Arrangement

Melodic house arrangements are highly structural. Because tracks in the 120–124 BPM range are typically designed to be mixed by DJs, they follow a predictable mathematical formula built on 16-bar and 32-bar macro-blocks.

Here is the standard structural anatomy of a melodic house track, moving from the DJ intro through the emotional peaks.



## 1. The DJ Intro (16 to 32 Bars)
* **Function:** A DJ mixing runway. It allows the previous track to smoothly transition into the new one.
* **Common Elements:** The 4/4 kick drum is present but often low-pass filtered. Percussion is sparse (perhaps just a closed hi-hat).
* **Melodic Cues:** The primary melodic motif is hinted at, usually played on a muted, heavily filtered synth or a delayed piano.
* **Atmosphere:** This is where foundational environmental layers—such as wind, water, or continuous Foley textures—are established without competing against heavy bass.

## 2. Verse / The Groove (16 to 32 Bars)
* **Function:** Establishing the core rhythm and the physical "head-nod" factor.
* **Common Elements:** The kick drum filter opens up. The sub-bass and mid-bass are introduced, anchoring the low frequencies. The primary 16th-note hi-hat groove begins.
* **Melodic Cues:** The lead synth is playing its core progression, but its low-pass filter remains relatively closed (e.g., around 400Hz). It feels restrained and submerged.
* **Energy Level:** Medium-low. The listener is locked into the groove, but the emotional peak is being withheld.

## 3. Build / Tension Phase (16 Bars)
* **Function:** Pushing the energy upward and signaling an imminent transition.
* **Common Elements:** Snare rolls or 16th-note claps are introduced. White noise risers begin to swell.
* **Automation:** Low-pass filters on the lead synths and basslines steadily open, revealing aggressive, bright upper harmonics. A high-pass filter often slowly cuts the low-end from the kick drum and bass to create a psychoacoustic lift.
* **The Vacuum (Final 1-2 Bars):** The "Wait for the Drop" hook occurs here. All drums and heavy synths abruptly cut to silence. A solitary, highly specific sound—such as a single vocal chop, a reversed cymbal, or an isolated ASMR nature hit—triggers in the empty space.



## 4. Chorus / Drop 1 (16 to 32 Bars)
* **Function:** The first emotional and physical release.
* **Common Elements:** The high-pass filters are disabled, bringing back the heavy sub-bass and punchy kick drum. All percussion, including the wide off-beat open hi-hats, plays simultaneously.
* **Melodic Cues:** The lead synth is fully open and unrestricted. It is wide, bright, and heavily sidechain-compressed to the kick drum, creating a massive, pumping wall of sound.
* **Atmosphere:** Organic environmental layers are pushed down in the mix, allowing the electronic synthesis to dominate.

## 5. The Breakdown / Bridge (16 to 32 Bars)
* **Function:** The emotional centerpiece of the track. It provides physical rest while maximizing melodic depth.
* **Common Elements:** The kick drum, sub-bass, and all driving percussion are completely removed.
* **Melodic Cues:** Lush, sustained chords and wide atmospheric pads expand to fill the stereo field. The primary arpeggio often plays out cleanly.
* **Atmosphere:** This section relies heavily on cinematic soundscapes. It is designed to invoke feelings of wonder, awe, or melancholic exploration.

## 6. Build 2 (16 Bars)
* **Function:** Ramping the energy back up for the final peak.
* **Common Elements:** Functions exactly like the first build, utilizing risers, filter sweeps, and a final vacuum. However, it often starts from a grander, more emotional place because the lush breakdown pads are still fading out.

## 7. Chorus / Drop 2 (32 Bars)
* **Function:** The maximum peak of the track.
* **Common Elements:** This mirrors Drop 1 but is typically larger.
* **Variations:** Producers often add a new element here to prevent repetition. This could be a new counter-melody, an arp playing one octave higher, or a heavier crash cymbal sequence on the downbeats.

## 8. Outro (16 to 32 Bars)
* **Function:** Subtractive arrangement to allow the DJ to mix into the next track.
* **Common Elements:** The track is methodically deconstructed. The heavy bass and lead synths are removed or slowly filtered back down. The groove strips back to just the kick drum and simple hi-hats, eventually fading out into the atmospheric noise layer.
