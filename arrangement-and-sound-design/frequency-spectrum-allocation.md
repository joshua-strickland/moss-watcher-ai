# Melodic House Frequency Spectrum Allocation (Instrumental)

Here is a comprehensive breakdown of frequency allocation for an instrumental melodic house mix.

In a track without vocals, the midrange (typically occupied by the human voice) is completely available. This allows your synth leads, midbasses, and atmospheric organic textures to dominate the central frequency spectrum, creating a wider, more immersive soundscape.

## 1. Sub Bass & Lows (20Hz – 60Hz)
This is the foundational energy of the track. It must be kept in mono and strictly controlled to prevent muddiness.
* **Sub-Bass:** Pure sine or triangle wave layers playing the fundamental root notes.
* **Kick Drum (Fundamental):** The primary thud and weight of the soft 4/4 kick drum.
* *Mixing Rule:* Nothing else belongs here. Pads, synth leads, Foley layers, and midbasses should be aggressively high-pass filtered to keep this region entirely clear for the kick and sub-bass.

## 2. Low-Mids (60Hz – 250Hz)
This range provides warmth and physical thickness, but too much buildup here causes the mix to sound "boxy" or muffled.
* **Mid-Bass (Fundamentals):** The lowest notes of the sawtooth or square wave bass layers.
* **Kick Drum (Body):** The "knock" of the kick drum.
* **Pads (Low End):** The absolute lowest frequencies of the sustained atmospheric chords.
* *Mixing Rule:* Sidechain compression is critical here. The mid-bass and pads must duck when the kick drum strikes to prevent low-mid frequency collisions.

## 3. Lower Midrange (250Hz – 500Hz)
This area contains the harmonic foundation of your melodic elements.
* **Synth Leads (Body):** The fundamental notes of the arpeggiated melodies.
* **Pads (Warmth):** The thick, dense portion of the analog synthesizer washes.
* **Percussion:** The weight of the claps, rimshots, and low-tuned organic wood blocks.
* *Mixing Rule:* Since there are no vocals, pads can be mixed slightly louder and wider in this range to create a deep, enveloping forest-like canopy.

## 4. Midrange (500Hz – 2.5kHz)
The most sensitive area of human hearing. In instrumental tracks, this is where the main hook commands attention.
* **Synth Leads (Presence):** The driving, aggressive character of the lead synth. With no vocal to compete against, the lead can comfortably sit dead center in this frequency band.
* **Mid-Bass (Harmonics):** The saturated grit and crunch of the mid-bass layer, allowing the bassline to be heard on smaller speakers or phone screens.
* **Foley & ASMR (Body):** The core sound of flowing water, wind, or dry leaves crunching.
* **Percussion:** The "snap" of the claps and the fundamental tone of closed hi-hats.

## 5. Upper Midrange (2.5kHz – 5kHz)
This range provides definition, attack, and clarity.
* **Synth Leads (Attack):** The pluck, initial transient, or "click" of the synthesizer envelope.
* **Pads (Clarity):** The higher, shimmering notes of the chord voicings.
* **Foley Elements (Detail):** The sharp, distinct textures of nature recordings, such as a solitary bird chirp or the high-frequency snap of a twig.
* *Mixing Rule:* Overcrowding this area causes ear fatigue. If the synth lead is particularly bright, apply a slight dynamic EQ cut to the pads in this range to let the lead cut through smoothly.

## 6. Highs & Air (5kHz – 20kHz)
This top end provides spatial width, breath, and the perception of a high-fidelity environment.
* **Cymbals & Shakers:** Off-beat open hi-hats, crash cymbals, and organic shakers.
* **Atmospheric Noise:** Continuous white noise layers and the high-frequency "hiss" of analog emulation VSTs.
* **Reverb & Delay Tails:** The long, shimmering reflections of the synth leads and pads stretching across the stereo field.
* **Foley (Air):** The highest, airy frequencies of wind or rain recordings.
* *Mixing Rule:* Keep this area wide and sparkling. Use a high-shelf EQ boost on the drum bus or the atmospheric bus to lift the "air" of the track without increasing the volume of the harsher mid-frequencies.
