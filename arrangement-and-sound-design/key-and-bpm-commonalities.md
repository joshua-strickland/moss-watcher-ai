Here is a detailed breakdown of the tempos and key signatures that define the melodic house genre, along with the technical reasons why they are utilized.

### Tempo (BPM)
The standard tempo for melodic house is tightly clustered within the **120 to 124 BPM** range.

* **The Groove:** This specific pocket provides enough forward momentum to maintain a danceable, four-on-the-floor house rhythm, but it is slow enough to prevent the intricate percussive elements from feeling rushed.
* **Acoustic Space:** A tempo of 120–124 BPM leaves substantial physical space between the kick drums. This gap is crucial for allowing lush, sweeping atmospheric pads, long reverb tails, and organic Foley textures to "breathe" and decay naturally before the next beat strikes.
* **The Physical Effect:** This range mirrors a brisk walking pace or an elevated resting heart rate, effectively matching the rhythm of an explorer moving steadily through a landscape.

### Key Signatures
Melodic house relies heavily on minor keys to establish its signature emotional resonance.



* **Minor Keys (The Core Sound):** Keys such as **Eb Minor** and **G Minor** are staples of the genre. Minor scales naturally evoke feelings of longing, introspection, or melancholy. When paired with driving, energetic percussion and opening low-pass filters, this creates the "melancholic but hopeful" atmosphere central to the genre's appeal.
* **Common Minor Keys:** * Eb Minor, F Minor, G Minor, A Minor, and C Minor.
* **Major Keys (The "Glow" Sound):** While less prevalent, major keys are deployed strategically to create purely uplifting, triumphant, or bright tracks. Keys like **Eb Major** or **C Major** are used when the emotional goal shifts from introspection to pure awe or warmth.

### The Sub-Bass Frequency Relationship
The selection of key signatures in melodic house is not solely an emotional choice; it is also a highly technical mixing decision related to sub-bass frequencies.

In electronic music, the sub-bass typically needs to sit cleanly in the **40Hz to 60Hz** range to provide physical impact on club sound systems without muddying the low-mid frequencies.
* The root note of **F1** sits at approximately 43Hz.
* The root note of **G1** sits at 49Hz.
* The root note of **Eb1** sits at around 38Hz (often pushing the lowest limits of standard sound systems).

Choosing keys like F Minor or G Minor allows the primary sub-bass note (the root) to hit exactly in the optimal frequency pocket, ensuring the track's foundation is powerful, deep, and physically resonant.
